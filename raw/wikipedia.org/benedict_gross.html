Benedict Hyman Gross (born June 22, 1950) is an American mathematician who is a professor at the University of California at San Diego,[1] the George Vasmer Leverett Professor of Mathematics Emeritus at Harvard University, and former Dean of Harvard College.[2]

He is known for his work in number theory, particularly the Gross–Zagier theorem on L-functions of elliptic curves, which he researched with Don Zagier.

Gross is a 1986 MacArthur Fellow.

Professional career[edit]

In 1971 he graduated Phi Beta Kappa from Harvard University. He then received an M.Sc. from Oxford University as a Marshall Scholar in 1974 before returning to Harvard and completing his Ph.D. in 1978, under John Tate.[2][3]

After holding faculty positions at Princeton University and Brown University, Gross became a tenured professor at Harvard in 1985[2] and remained there subsequently, as Dean of Harvard College from 2003 to 2007.[4] He was elected as a fellow of the American Academy of Arts and Sciences in 1992[5] and as a member of the National Academy of Sciences in 2004.[6]

One of his past Ph.D. students was Noam Elkies.[3]

Benedict Gross was the mathematical consultant[7] for the 1980 film It's My Turn containing the famous scene[8] in which Jill Clayburgh impeccably proves the snake lemma.

Awards and honors[edit]

Gross, Zagier, and Dorian M. Goldfeld won the Cole Prize of the American Mathematical Society in 1987 for their work on the Gross–Zagier theorem.[9] In 2012 he became a fellow of the American Mathematical Society.[10] He was elected to the American Philosophical Society in 2017[11].
